Non-Bound Gods & Beings
Layer 3 Residents · Virtue Demons · Sin Devils
The thirteen Bound Gods reside on the Material Plane under the Gods' Law. Beyond them, divinity continues: Layer-3 gods whose portfolios reach Talan from across the veil, virtue-demons that perversion-mirror the moral catalogue, sin-devils that embody the sins themselves. They are not bound by the Compact; mortal clerics on Talan still channel through several of them.
Named Non-Bound Gods
Layer 3 Residents · Belief-Shaped · Sancta-less on Talan

The Layer 3 (Postlife) layer holds many gods beyond the Thirteen. Most go unnamed in Talanese sources — their portfolios are unspecific, their cults are local, their pantheons unmapped. The four below are the named ones whose portfolios appear in clerical practice on Talan: a Major God of the moon, two Minor Gods of indigenous lineage and soul-routing, and a Minor God of Undeath who was once mortal himself. All four are belief-shaped the same way the Ascendant Six are — they take their form from mortal worship and folk practice.

None maintains a sanctum on Talan. None walks the Material Plane. Faith reaches them across the veil; they answer through grants and dreams.

Major GodDiyu — Layer 3 · Neutral
Bikiargi
The Twins — Moon · Tides · Lunar Omens · Dreams
Aspects
Moonlight · Tides · Lunar Omens · The Dream-Currents · Lovers' Meetings · Hunts in the Dark
Worshippers
Sailors, navigators, witches, lovers, the Iron Tide's astronomers, midwives (births tracked by moon-phase), nocturnal hunters
Etymology
Basque biki (twin) + argi (light) → bikiargi "the twin-light"

Two beings, one portfolio. Always depicted as a paired figure, never alone. The two share the lunar portfolio absolutely; mortals praying for moon-tied outcomes invoke "Bikiargi," not one twin or the other.

The Bright Twin is benevolent — calm tides, safe roads under moonlight, true dreams, lovers reunited, the moonlit hunt that comes home well-fed. The Dark Twin is malevolent — ill omens, hunting weather for what shouldn't be hunted, false dreams, sleepwalkers led to cliffs, the moonlit hunt that becomes the hunted.

Together they are neutral, which is why they reside in Diyu. Mortals who try to invoke only one inevitably draw both. The Twins do not separate. Iron Tide ships habitually keep separate shrines to each twin.

Mortals have prayed to the two moons (Veyru and Calune) for as long as there have been mortals. The prayer made the gods — same mechanism as the Ascendant Six writ small.
Minor GodDiyu — Layer 3 · Neutral
Zaharsuge
The Eldest — Wyrmkin · Indigenous Serpents · Primal Wyrms
Aspects
Indigenous serpents, drakes, basilisks, primal wyrms · the deep-earth slither · the pre-Dragon lineage of Talan's scaled beasts
Worshippers
Dragonets above all (his direct ancestral children); also lizardfolk, nagaji, naga ancestry, some kobolds (producing a religious schism with Komo's kobold-incarnation lore), small dragon-blood cults that reject Dragon's Reach's authority claim
Etymology
Basque suge (serpent) + zahar (ancient) → zahar-suge "the ancient one of serpents"

Predates the alien Dragons by ages. Zaharsuge's wyrm-line was the established serpentine intelligence of Talan when the Dragon mothership crashed during the Gods' Era. The alien Dragons' "we are the true wyrm-lords" claim is, on Talan, theologically wrong — Zaharsuge was here first, and many of his descendants know it.

Sancta-less on Talan, but his shadow falls heaviest in the upper Darklands — the Sentinel Dwarves who patrol there may be unknowingly guarding his cultic sites. His living lineage on Talan are the Dragonets, small dragonlike sapients concentrated in the upper Darklands and the Basogur Jungle.

Theological hook: indigenous wyrm-cultists have a grievance against the Dragons of Dragon's Reach. Many Dragonets refuse to recognise Dragon's Reach as having authority over wyrmkind on Talan. Untested, simmering.
Minor GodDiyu — Layer 3 · Neutral
Epairima
The Judge of Souls — Verdict · Weighing · Sorting
Aspects
Verdict · Weighing · Sorting · Second-Chance Allocation · Soul-Routing
Worshippers
The bereaved, the dying, executioners, jurists who want their work to outlast them, anyone whose loved one died unclaimed
Etymology
Basque epai (judgment) + arima (soul) → epai-arima "soul-judgment"

Resides in Diyu — specifically in Dauria, the City of the Dead, where her court is the central feature. Every soul that reaches Dauria unclaimed (no god has called them onward) is weighed at her court. Her verdict routes the soul: onward to a claimant god, onward to the Wellspring, or — rarely — back to the layer above for a second pass at the life they failed.

Not the only judge in existence, but she is the named judge whose office is invoked in mortal funerary rites on Talan. Other judges presumably operate elsewhere; Talanese clergy invoke Epairima by name.

Minor GodAbyss — Layer 3 · Evil
Betibizi
The Self-Ascended — Undeath · The Original Integration-Procedure Holder
Aspects
Undeath · Refusal of Death · The Prolongation of the Self Past Its Natural Span · Necromantic Continuity
Worshippers
Necromantic underground · ascension-cultists · surviving Storveldi-Denbora-descended lineages (most of whom inherited the false Elden-blood claim and don't know whose godhood they're invoking) · the desperate ill · those who refuse to grieve
Etymology
Basque betiko (eternal/forever) + bizi (life, alive) → betiko-bizi "eternal life." Self-named at the moment of ascension — the name is a declaration, not a gift.

Origin: he was once mortal. A member of the Storveldi Denbora's ascended ruling class who survived the Week of Crimson Rain's annihilation of his homeland (the cursed-ground scar now called the Lost Kingdom) and used the integration procedure on himself in the aftermath, ascending to Minor God status. He fled Talan for Abyss and has not returned.

He is the original holder of the integration procedure — the technique for imbuing a found shard of divinity into a mortal's own divine essence — and the reason it persists at all. Fragments persist on Talan among his cult and the Azarketi-descended lineages; reconstructing the complete procedure is the largest unsolved capability gap on Talan.

The Blackened Lands' natural-born Skeleton ancestry and the region's ambient necromantic resonance trace spiritually back to Betibizi, though the proximate cause is the cursed ground itself (the divine retaliation that destroyed his civilisation). Necromancers on Talan who reach for life-after-life magic are reaching for his domain, whether they know his name or not.

Theological opposition: Betibizi stands in direct theological opposition to Epairima. She sorts and releases the dead; he keeps them, recursively, against the natural flow. Their portfolios were not designed to be at odds — but they are.

Grants the Undeath domain to clerics on Talan. Full integration-procedure context on magic.html → Mortal Ascent Ladder; full Storveldi history on history.html → Gods' Era.
Open Thread — The Sun

Mortals have prayed to Solyra for as long as there have been mortals. The same mechanism that birthed Bikiargi from prayers to the two moons should plausibly have produced a sun-deity from prayers to the sun. Such a god is not yet named or detailed in canon — they may exist as a Major God of the Sun (paired-mate to the Twins by symmetry), may exist as a single deity with no twinship, or may have been absorbed by Iro's Light portfolio long enough ago that the distinct sun-god dissolved back into the Wellspring. Canon-pending.

Virtue Demons — The Aristotelian Pattern
One Demon per Virtue's Corruption · Excess or Deficiency

Talan's demons follow a coherent theological pattern that mortal scholars have catalogued: each demon is the personification of a virtue's corruption. Following the classical doctrine of the mean, every virtue sits between two vices — one of excess, one of deficiency. A demon embodies one of those two extremes.

This is why the demons are not random or symmetric: they are exactly as numerous as there are virtue/extreme pairs in the Talanese moral tradition, and they map cleanly to those pairs.

Named so far · Muiral the Misshapen
The only individually named virtue demon in canon. Muiral is a Modesty-line demon (Shamelessness / Shyness pair) and grants the Abomination domain — corrupted Modesty either dissolves form (Shyness extreme) or inflates it into grotesque self-display (Shamelessness extreme); either produces the misshapen. Muiral appears in mortal exorcism texts and in a handful of Adventurers' Guild high-rank contract reports. The rest of the virtue lines have demons too — they just have not yet named themselves.
Implications
Demons of opposed extremes within the same virtue line are theological enemies as well as cosmic ones — the Cowardice-demon and the Rashness-demon both pervert Courage, and both have reasons to hate each other. Mortal philosophers who study the pattern can predict demon behaviour; a handful of Enki-aligned exorcists work by reading the demon, identifying its line, then deploying the opposite extreme to push it off-pattern.
Sin Devils — The Seven
One Devil-Lord per Sin · Seven Lords, No More, No Less

The devils of Talan are organised on a simpler pattern: the Seven Sins. Unlike the virtue demons (which corrupt something good), sin devils embody the sins themselves directly. One canonical devil-lord per sin — seven sins, seven lords. The structural pattern is locked; the names of the lords are mostly canon-pending.

Three sin-devils are bound to grantable cleric domains on Talan via the Domains Outside the Thirteen mapping. The other four grantable domains, and all seven devil-lord names, remain open.

Demon & Devil — the Talan Distinction

Demons corrupt a virtue. Their existence is parasitic on the virtue line — perversions of what could have been good.

Devils embody a sin. Their existence is foundational — the sin given form.

Both are redeemable in principle. The virtue line still runs through every demon, and every devil retains the seed of the contrary virtue. Redemption work is rare and brutally difficult, but it is canonically possible — no being on Talan is fixed beyond change.

Demons proliferate around mortals who have already half-corrupted a virtue. Devils proliferate around mortals who choose a sin.

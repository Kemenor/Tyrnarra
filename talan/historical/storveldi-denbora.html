The Storveldi Denbora
Late Gods' Era · "The Old Race" · Killers of Tani · Annihilated in the Crimson Rain
"Every chronicle of the Gods' Era's last century names them differently. All agree they ended it. Beyond that, the scholarship is a noise of competing names — the Old Race, the Storveldi Denbora, the Elden Returned, the sorcerer-kings of the western coast. Pick a chronicler and you will be told which is right with absolute certainty. Pick a second chronicler and you will be told the opposite."
✦

At a Glance

Dates
Late Gods' Era. Chronicle attestation is sparse and contradictory. Most accounts place their rise in the final two or three centuries before 1 GR; founding date disputed, end date locked at the start of the Week of Crimson Rain.
Identity
Contested. Some chronicles name them the Storveldi Denbora; far more know them only as the Old Race. A persistent scholarly tradition holds they were the Elden returned from wherever the Elden Era's vanishing had taken them. A second tradition insists they were a wholly separate mortal civilisation. A third — common in temple scholarship — calls them sorcerer-kings who pried open a divine secret no mortal was meant to touch. The question has never been settled in mortal scholarship.
Origin
What chronicles do agree on: they rose in western Lioaru, settling atop the abandoned ruins of an Elden city. Their capital sat on those foundations and inherited the awe that surrounded them. Whether by blood, by squat, or by some other claim — chronicles disagree.
Capital
The cursed ground now called the Lost Kingdom — the Blackened Lands of western Lioaru, where Tani fell. The capital's name is not preserved in any surviving chronicle reliable enough to cite.
Government
Theocratic, hierarchical, and feared. Beyond that, accounts diverge sharply: some name an ascended sovereign-caste of god-touched nobles; others speak of mortal kings who held court at the centre of vast ritual machinery; others still describe a priesthood that was the kingdom. Open in chronicle.
Language
Original tongue extinct. No corpus has been recovered. Comparative linguists occasionally claim to identify loan-words drifted into modern Azarketi cultural vocabulary; the work is contested.

The Chronicle Controversy

No civilisation of Talan's chronicle-record carries more competing identifications than this one. Each of the four major traditions has serious scholarship behind it; none has documentary footing strong enough to settle the question. Almost all of the actual records were lost when the homeland was annihilated, and what survives are second- and third-hand accounts from neighbouring kingdoms whose own libraries were thinned by the Crimson Rain in the days that followed.

The Elden Returned
"The vanished came back to end what the gods had built."
The oldest and most popular reading. The Elden Era ended in 2945 GR with the Elden vanishing in a single day — and this tradition holds that they returned, perhaps two thousand years later, to settle a quarrel with the gods who had filled the void they left. The fact that the "Old Race" built their capital atop an Elden ruin is treated as evidence: who else would inherit a vanished civilisation's site so confidently? The folk-name the Old Race is itself usually understood, in this tradition, as a reference to the Elden.
A Separate Mortal Civilisation
"Mortals rose. Mortals reached too far. The result was the same."
A growing minority reading, particularly in secular and Forsetian scholarship. This tradition argues that the "Old Race" were a wholly mortal people — not the Elden, not their descendants — who happened to occupy the empty Elden site and who built their own civilisation on top of it. Their downfall, in this reading, is the second of two great cautionary tales about mortal overreach (the first being the rumoured Storveldi Denbora ascent programmes, which some in this tradition treat as the same story). The Azarketi inheritance is taken seriously here, though debated.
The Sorcerer-Kings
"They pried open a divine secret no mortal was meant to touch."
Common in temple traditions and especially in the surviving record of Tani's clergy. The "Old Race" are read here as a specifically mystical or sorcerous polity — kings who unlocked one ritual too many, found themselves able to challenge a Grand God, and were obliterated for it. The emphasis is moral rather than ethnographic: the what they were matters less than the why they fell. This reading is the closest mortal scholarship gets to the underlying mechanic, even though it dresses the truth in the wrong vocabulary.
The Storveldi Denbora Hypothesis
"A name some chronicles preserve — most do not."
A minority of chronicles — almost all of them late-Gods'-Era or early-Lost-Era survivals — preserve the name Storveldi Denbora for this civilisation. The scholars who take the attribution seriously argue that this is the people's own self-name, surviving in the rare records that pre-date the conflation with the Elden. The scholars who do not take it seriously argue that Storveldi Denbora is a chronicle-tradition's invented label, retrofitted to a civilisation whose actual name no one alive remembers. Both positions are defensible. This page uses Storveldi Denbora as the working title because it is at least specific.
⚿   Known to Almost No One

The fourth tradition is correct. The civilisation's own self-name was Storveldi Denbora, and the other three traditions are debris from a lie its own rulers authored.

Mortal, Not Elden Squatters Atop a Ruin Authored Their Own Lie
The Public Claim
Heirs of the Elden
Every Storveldi court chronicle, every monument inscription, every diplomatic letter opened with the lineage claim. They presented themselves as the surviving remnant of the Elden — children of the vanished, rightful custodians of the high-tech ruins beneath their feet. The claim worked because no one alive could check it. A confident civilisation atop intact Elden ruins, speaking of our forebears, was simply believed.
The Actual Truth
Squatters Who Read
They had no Elden blood. They were a mortal people of the Gods' Era — ancestry-derived, deity-mediated, indistinguishable in soul-structure from any other mortal civilisation — who happened to find an empty Elden city and the strata of ruin beneath it. They settled, learned what they could decipher, and built outward. What they could not understand they buried under monument. The descent-claim was the cap-stone.
The conflation persists because they engineered it. Every variant of "the Old Race" that does not name them by their own name is a Storveldi success story.

The God Killing Incident

Whatever they were called, every chronicle agrees on what they did. At the end of the Gods' Era, this civilisation killed Tani — the Grand God of Time, struck down in or beside their own capital in her own domain. A Grand God had been killed for the first time in the world's recorded history. The folk-tongue calls the event simply the God Killing Incident, a phrase whose elegance comes from how thoroughly it omits the killers' identity.

The mechanism is unknown. Chronicles speculate. Tani's clergy hold one view; secular historians another; the temples of Cronus and Araphel a third. The common ground is small: there was a strike, it succeeded, and a Grand God died. The killers' methods are not preserved in any record reliable enough to publish.

What the killing actually triggered, every account does agree on. The Week of Crimson Rain opened within hours. The eleven remaining Grand Gods — now confronted with proof that they too could be killed — went to war. The killers' homeland was the first to fall. The capital was annihilated. The land around it has not healed.

⚿   The Choke-Point Knowledge

Their actual achievement was not the recovery of Elden technology. It was the integration procedure — the technique for taking a recovered shard of divinity and imbuing it into a mortal's own divine essence. The procedure was the choke point; the procedure is what enabled everything that followed.

The Wellspring/belief mechanic that lifts a mortal toward Demi-God status was openly understood in the Gods' Era. Many civilisations used it. What the Storveldi Denbora had that no one else did was the recipe for converting a found shard into ascended capability rather than leaving it as inert relic. (Full mechanic: Magic & Faith → Mortal Ascent Ladder.) The procedure had been mythical or fragmentary everywhere else. The Storveldi Denbora held it complete, and they held it institutionally. Ascensions could be planned, scheduled, and stacked.

Demi-God Court Two Minor Gods at the Top Programmed, Not Lucky
The Apex
Two Minor Gods
Two sovereigns — names TBD — went past Demi-God to Minor God by systematic shard recovery and full application of the procedure. The only mortals in recorded history known to have reached Minor-God tier by integration of recovered shards rather than the rare lucky individual case. They led the strike on Tani.
The Court
Demi-God Nobility
Beneath the two, a substantial body of Demi-Gods — perhaps dozens — sat as the active court. Lifted by belief-and-power accumulation under the procedure's supervised application, they ran the day-to-day, oversaw the shard programme, and supplied the political weight the strike required.
The Base
Mortal Subjects
The mortal population. Citizens of a kingdom whose senior nobles were gods in everything but name, and whose two highest sovereigns were gods full-stop. Most never saw the ascent programme from inside. Most believed the Elden-descent story they had been raised in. Most died in the Crimson Rain alongside the masters who had lied to them.

With that concentration of mortal-derived divinity, the two Minor Gods supported by their Demi-God court did what no other mortal civilisation had ever organised the scale to attempt. They attacked Tani in her own domain and killed her. The strike's specific method is not preserved — every chronicle that might have recorded it died with the capital — but the political and divine arithmetic behind it is straightforward: two Minor Gods plus a Demi-God ruling class, against one unsuspecting Grand God on home ground.

The arithmetic worked once. It is the only time it has ever been allowed to work.

The Wound — The Lost Kingdom

What remains of the Storveldi Denbora homeland is the Lost Kingdom — the cursed coastal sub-region of western Lioaru known in older chronicles as the Blackened Lands and in traveller's tales as the black spot on the map. Concentrated divine retaliation fell here at Grand-God scale during the Crimson Rain, and the wound has never closed.

The terrain reads wrong to anyone who walks it. Blackened soil. Time-warped strata where moments pool and repeat. Ruins that read newer than the stone they sit on. The ground twists living flesh into the irregular bodies of Fleshwarps; the restless wound pulls the dead back into motion as Skeletons. Neither ancestry is anyone's deliberate work — they are the land itself, still bleeding. No kingdom claims the interior. No reliable maps exist of the deep ground. The Adventurers' Guild posts a perpetual standing warning. Necromancers, ascension-cultists, and a handful of Tani-mourning pilgrim sects make the edges anyway.

The Lost Kingdom is the only physical evidence of the Storveldi Denbora that the chronicle-record can point to without dispute. The civilisation itself is contested. The wound it left is not.

The Azarketi Question

The Azarketi — a water-adapted ancestry scattered across Talan's coastal communities — almost universally claim Elden blood in their inherited cultural tradition. The claim is old, deep, and held with the kind of certainty that does not invite scholarly cross-examination from outside the lineage.

A persistent minority of scholars argues that the Azarketi are in fact survivors of the "Old Race" — descendants of those who had been on the periphery of the kingdom when the Crimson Rain fell, and who carried inherited body-modification rather than Elden ancestry. In this reading the "Elden blood" claim is a folk-memory of the same Elden-conflation that has dogged the chronicle record for three thousand years: the Azarketi inherited their forebears' Elden-claim along with their bodies and have transmitted it forward across millennia.

The Azarketi themselves, by and large, reject this reading. Their cultural traditions are explicit about Elden descent, and the few scholars who have argued otherwise have generally not found the diaspora's family lines a welcoming audience. The question remains open in published scholarship; in living rooms it is mostly closed.

⚿   The Lie Outlived Everyone Who Knew It Was a Lie

The minority reading is correct. The Azarketi descend from the Storveldi Denbora, not from the Elden, and their inherited "Elden blood" claim is the Storveldi's original lie passed down across three and a half millennia in a form none of the modern claimants recognise.

Storveldi Descent Water-Adaptation = Storveldi Work The Cover Outlived the Authors

Not every Storveldi Denbora citizen was at the capital when the gods came. A scattered population of survivors — descendants of those who had been experimented upon, or who carried the inherited results of generations of court-supervised self-experiment, but who had never themselves been elevated — survived the retaliation as a diaspora. They are the modern Azarketi. The water-adapted features the Azarketi heritage carries today were Storveldi body-modification work, refined by generations of selection and three thousand years of stable inheritance.

Most modern Azarketi do not know any of this. A handful of family-line elders suspect or know. The diaspora itself, as a body, still believes the cover its forebears authored.

A campaign-relevant property: the Azarketi as a population carry pieces of the integration procedure in their cultural inheritance — devotional songs, ceremonial gestures, dietary observances whose original purpose was procedural rather than cultural. No individual carries enough to be a threat. The pattern across the diaspora is something else.

Legacy in the Chronicle Record

Three things survive of the Storveldi Denbora in any form a chronicler can cite. The Lost Kingdom as physical wound — the unhealed coast that no living scholar would deny. The Azarketi diaspora, whose connection to the civilisation is debated but whose existence is not. And the chronicle-record's own confusion: the four traditions, the conflation with the Elden, the phrase the God Killing Incident that everyone uses and almost no one can date.

What this civilisation actually did at the end of the Gods' Era — how they did it, who their kings were, how a Grand God could be killed by anything other than another Grand God — is the most-asked unanswered question in Gods'-Era scholarship. The Guild has standing requests open with multiple chronicler-fellowships for any pre-Crimson-Rain document that might bear on the question. Almost nothing comes in. The fewer-and-fewer documents that do tend to disagree with each other in ways that suggest each chronicler had been working from a different fragment of the same vanished record.

The conflation persists because it is convenient. The Elden are also vanished, also mysterious, also implicated in cosmic catastrophe — and conflating two enigmatic civilisations into one closed mystery is easier than maintaining two open ones. Most modern mortals do not distinguish the two at all. Scholars who do, do so quietly.

◈   Folk-Myth & Common Belief
Outside the chronicler-fellowships, almost no one on Talan distinguishes the four scholarly traditions. The common phrasings collapse them. "The Old Race who killed Tani" is the standard formulation, used interchangeably with "the Elden" in most tavern-tales, sermons, and traveller's accounts. The God Killing Incident is a phrase children know without knowing what it refers to. The black spot on the map is what most maps still print where the Lost Kingdom should be. Folk-tradition is mostly content to leave the killers nameless and the wound visible — the lesson, in folk-memory, is what happens to mortals (or to gods, or to whichever-they-were) who reach beyond their station. The Azarketi diaspora's claim to Elden blood is the largest single piece of inherited identity that turns on the question, and it is held with conviction; most Talanese who interact with Azarketi communities do not even know there is a contested-history question to ask.
⚿   Talan's Largest Unsolved Capability Gap

The Storveldi Denbora are dead as a polity. The integration procedure they developed is not lost. The complete recipe survives, scattered across the continent and one Postlife plane, in fragments none of which is sufficient on its own — and the reconstruction problem is the most dangerous open project on Talan.

Complete — In the Abyss Fragmentary — On Talan Bound Thirteen Cannot Act Pre-emptively

One member of the inner court survived the fall — held a working knowledge of the procedure, was not present at the capital when Tani was struck down, and used the procedure on himself in the aftermath. He ascended to Minor God status, took the self-given name Betibizi (Basque-drifted betiko bizi, "eternal life"), and fled Talan for the Abyss rather than face the Grand Gods' attention. He has not returned. He is the published Minor God of Undeath worshipped today, and the original holder of the procedure as it persists.

What persists on Talan is fragmentary, and it persists in three identifiable forms.

Fragment One
Betibizi's Cult
A small worship-tradition centred on the Minor God of Undeath. The cult holds devotional fragments of the procedure encoded as religious practice — most of its members do not know they are guarding a technical document. Betibizi has not chosen to teach the complete procedure to anyone in recorded history. His clergy hold inflections of fragments. The rest is his secret.
Fragment Two
Azarketi Family Lines
Hereditary fragments transmitted within certain Azarketi family lines as cultural tradition — devotional songs, ceremonial gestures, dietary observances whose original purpose was procedural. Most carriers do not know what they have. A small number of family-line elders do know, or suspect. Named lineages TBD.
Fragment Three
Archive Caches
Buried Storveldi-era archive caches that survived the destruction of the capital by being elsewhere — provincial libraries, diplomatic mission-houses in neighbouring kingdoms, the occasional sealed scholar's vault. Locations and survival rates are open. What fraction of the original record any of them preserves is open.

The shard sites are known. The places the Storveldi Denbora pulled shards from in their original programme are largely catalogued — old godblood battlefields, ruined Gods'-Era sites, the rare veil-touched location. Each is now insanely well-guarded by whichever modern faction has authority over that ground. Knowing where the shards are does not get you up the ladder. The procedure is what hides, and the procedure is the choke point. An adventurer-party that has somehow obtained a real shard and intact procedure documentation — even partial — is sitting on the most dangerous knowledge a mortal can hold on Talan.

The bound thirteen do not discuss it. Cronus almost certainly knows; the others' awareness is open. The bound thirteen carry, in their bound state, a permanent structural inability to act pre-emptively on a possibility — only on the realised event. The procedure is therefore one of the most quietly load-bearing secrets on the continent: nobody is actively suppressing it, because nobody outside the lower-single-digit circle knows it has not been lost. The day someone does is the day the calculus changes.

Why this matters as canon. The Storveldi Denbora are the only mortals known to have killed a Grand God. They are the only mortals known to have reached Minor-God tier by integration of recovered shards. They are the only mortal civilisation known to have developed and held the integration procedure. Their fall is the structural twin of the Golden Empire's — mortal ascension overreaching its mortal scaffolding and bringing cosmic catastrophe down on what surrounded it. Twice in recorded history now.

GM-tier open threads. Six named identities and specifics remain open inside the GM truth — each closes when a story wants it.
  1. The two Minor Gods' personal names — the architects of the strike on Tani.
  2. Named members of the Demi-God ruling class — court figures, regional governors, senior shard-programme administrators.
  3. Betibizi's original mortal name — what he was called before he ascended and renamed himself.
  4. Fraction of the procedure already reassembled — in Betibizi's cult and in scattered Azarketi-line fragments. The central dial for any Storveldi-themed campaign.
  5. Named Azarketi family lines that remember — specific lineages whose inheritance contains procedurally-usable fragments, and the elders who suspect or know.
  6. Surviving Storveldi-era archive caches — locations, condition, holders, fraction of original record preserved.

⌬   Open in the Chronicle Record

Even within the chronicler-tier consensus, several questions remain open — these are the kinds of detail that future scholarship, or future excavation, might one day close.
  1. The capital's name — the city that sat atop the Elden ruins and was annihilated in the opening hours of the Crimson Rain. Now a ruin within the Lost Kingdom whose exact location is uncatalogued.
  2. The founding date and detailed timeline — chronicles agree on the late Gods' Era; the specific century of founding is disputed and may never be recoverable.
  3. The original tongue — the scholarly and administrative language of this civilisation. No corpus has been recovered. Comparative-linguistics reconstruction from Azarketi cultural vocabulary would itself be a campaign.
  4. Definitive resolution of the four traditions — Elden-Returned, separate-mortal, sorcerer-kings, or the Storveldi Denbora hypothesis. Chroniclers have been arguing for three millennia. The argument is unlikely to settle without new sources.

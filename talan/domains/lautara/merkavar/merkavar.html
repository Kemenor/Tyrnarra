Merkavar
The Market City · Jianna's God-City
The goddess of Commerce walks her own Market City, and sometimes she walks it as a customer. It is the fairest market on the continent, because the stranger at your stall might be Her.
✦

At a Glance

Etymology
Basque merkatu (market) + drift → Merkavar, "the Market." The byname (The Market City) is the gloss the rest of Talan reads.
Position
Central Lautara. An independent city-state-with-territory: the city, the great inland lake that surrounds it, and Helgafjall, the sacred snow-capped peak that rises from the lake's centre. Authority extends to the ring of water and the sacred peak. Surrounded by Azkataria (the philosopher-market and agricultural belt to the south, governed by the Open Floor and holding the southern pilgrim route up Helgafjall) and the rest of Lautara beyond.
Character
The continent's busiest commercial junction. Loud, dense, multi-ancestry, never closed.
Market CitySilk-Road HubSeasonal AssemblyCosmopolitan Clergy
Bound God
Jianna, domain of Commerce. Depicted as a vishkanya.
Governance
Two complementary institutions: the Seasonal Assembly (the legislature, meeting at each of the four great markets) and Jianna's clergy (the standing administration, year-round). Premise: "You are a citizen of Merkavar when you are here, and you govern it when you show up."
Peoples
Cosmopolitan; a Merkavar warehouse-floor at peak hour holds half the continent in one room. Three ancestries are politically significant: Vishkanya (over-represented in clergy and craft guilds), Halfling (the train crews and rail-operations bloc, organised under the Halfling Railworkers' Association), Kitsune (an ancient parallel tradition to Emarrea's Heartcourt, market-readers and information brokers).
Magitrain
The central hub of the southern continental network. The rail lines converge here; rolling stock manufactured by the Order of Steam reaches the network via the Rika Tikur ports, crosses by Lautaran water and road, and enters service at Merkavar to fan out across Atarialda toward Egulon, Ezkudon, and Zuzental.

The City, the Lake, and Helgafjall

The lake-city is a destination, not a port. Ships reach the Midarra coast at Rika Tikur or the Dreaming Cape, goods transfer to river-craft or caravan-train, and arrive at Merkavar to be sorted, valued, and redistributed onward. The continent's overland commerce passes through here at some point in its journey, and the city is built for it: freight yards along the lake's eastern shore, warehouse districts radiating outward, market-squares along the western shore for the seasonal markets, and the temple district climbing the lake-facing slopes toward Helgafjall.

Helgafjall (Icelandic helgr "holy" + fjall "mountain") is the sacred-mountain watcher over the market-city. Pilgrim trails wind up its slopes; the snow on its peak never fully melts, and the lake at its foot has never frozen even in the hardest winters. Pilgrims climb in every season; the harder the climb, the more honoured the prayer at the summit. Merchant-houses are said to send a senior apprentice up Helgafjall before any deal of consequence, on the theory that the goddess watches commerce more closely when the mountain is in the prayer.

The chronicler's standing guess for both the unmelting snow and the unfreezing water is the simplest one: that the goddess never truly leaves her city, and the two are the trace of a resident god.

The God Goes to Market

Merkavar's first fact is the one the rest of the continent forgets to say aloud: this is Jianna's own city, and a god walks their city-state with their own eyes. So in the Market City the goddess of Commerce is, sometimes, a customer. The veiled woman testing the silk at a stall, the stranger haggling over grain and paying without a quibble, might be Her, and every merchant in Merkavar half-believes it of the next person in line. She smiled at me once is a fortune made and a story a family tells for three generations.

The deepest devotion here is a fair deal struck with a stranger who might be the goddess, and Merkavar is the fairest market on the continent for exactly that reason: you short no one their weight, because the one you cheat could be the one whose smile lifts or breaks your house. Here the theology and the commercial ethics are a single rule.

The Plazas

The lake-shore city trades across many market-plazas, each with its own crowd and character. The travellers' plazas sit by the freight-yards and the caravan-train depots, loud with strangers and four tongues, their stock turning over with each season's arrivals. The local plazas of the permanent quarters are where the same families have kept the same stalls for lifetimes, and everyone knows the weight of everyone's hand. Others run by trade: the cloth-plaza, the grain-plaza, the foxfire-lit information-stalls where the kitsune sit. A merchant's plaza is half their identity, and the daily current of the city is the movement between them.

The abundance of squares is also why the goddess can never be pinned to one place. There is always another plaza she might be standing in today, and a city of squares to choose among leaves no corner where a crowd could reliably wait for her.

The Open Hand and the Smile-Touched

Jianna's image holds one hand open in offering and one closed, and the Merkavar merchant shows the open hand, palm out, to every stranger who reaches the stall, before a word of price. It means both I deal you fair and I greet you as Her, in case you are, and a child learns the gesture before they learn to count change. A merchant who keeps both hands on his goods is read at a glance as rude, or as hiding a thumb on the scale.

A family that caught the goddess's smile in passing is smile-touched, a phrase the whole city knows. Their fortune is said to lift, and often visibly does, which is the only evidence anyone ever has, because the clergy refuse to register or certify a smile: you cannot audit the goddess, and a temple-stamped smile would be a closed hand laid on an open gift. So the city is full of quiet smile-claims, some glowing true and some threadbare, the difference read only in the ledger of what came after.

Shorting a customer their weight is, in Merkavar, nearer to blasphemy than to crime. The one you cheated might have been the goddess testing the scale, so a caught cheat loses his licence to the assembly and his standing to the temple both, and the second wound is the one that does not heal. Under the roar of spice and lake-damp runs a constant half-attention, the whole market watching, just slightly, for the veiled woman who pays without haggling and is gone before you look up.

◈   Tavern-Tales of the Passing Smile
Every dockside tavern in Merkavar keeps its own roster of smile-touched houses, and the telling grows in the keeping. They say the brass-fitting family on the lower grain-plaza were nobodies until a veiled woman bought a candlestick off the eldest daughter, smiled, and walked off into the crowd, and within three years they held the plaza's best frontage. They say you cannot ask for the smile; the moment you arrange your face to deserve it, she has already passed you by. They say she favours the stallholder who gives a fair count to the beggar as readily as to the lord, and that more than one fortune was made by a kindness the giver thought no one saw. The skeptics, mostly clergy, note that the families who claim the smile are also the families who keep an honest scale and a long memory, and that fair dealing has a way of looking like luck after thirty years. The tavern-tellers answer that this is exactly what a smile-touched house would look like, and order another round.
⚿   Known to No One in Merkavar

Once a month, the goddess keeps a stall. The smile-touched belief is the city's unwitting record of it, holding the true effect and the wrong cause.

The Stall
Disguised, in one plaza or another and never the same twice, Jianna sets out a spread of trinkets and baubles and sells them with her own hands. The prices seem wrong to anyone watching, because she is not pricing the baubles. She is pricing the buyer.
The Smile Is the Sale
A family does not catch a smile in passing and then prosper. The family bought from her stall, honoured what they bought, and prospered. Mortals hold the effect and the wrong cause, and no one connects the strange-priced trinket to the rising luck.
The Trinkets Are Real
Each bauble is a genuine minor blessing of Jianna: true fortune to one who keeps and honours it, inert in the hands of one who would flip it, waste it, or forget it. She screens her buyers because the blessing is profaned on the careless. She is not selling the object; she is placing the blessing where it will be honoured.
The Price Is the Test
Sometimes absurdly cheap, to see whether you treasure a thing that cost you nothing; sometimes steep for a trinket, to see whether you will pay dearly for a worth you can only sense. The haggler has already failed: talking her down is pricing the object, the opposite of honouring it. She watches the eyes, the hands, the reason it is wanted.
She Cannot Be Hunted
Never the same day, never the same plaza, no pattern to stake out, and a city of squares to choose among. Were the stall predictable, every careless soul in Merkavar would camp it wearing a borrowed look of reverence and the test would die. The unpredictability keeps the screening honest. A refused buyer hears only "this one is not for you," and the stall is gone when they think to return.
No One Knows
The clergy do not; their doctrine that Jianna rewards the honouring heart is true without their grasping how literally. The smile-touched belief is the closest the city comes. Perhaps one or two very old merchants half-remember a woman who priced a brass bird strangely and looked at them too hard, and never let the thought finish.

The Two Faces, and the Whole of Her

Jianna appears in Merkavar two ways. Openly and rarely, at the great civic moments, a market's opening or a temple rite, she stands as herself, recognised, and the city stops; these are the witnessed theophanies the clergy keep and the chronicles record. Hidden and often, she walks the plazas veiled and unrecognised, the customer who might be anyone, and these are never confirmed.

The clergy's institutional faith leans on the open goddess of rite and law; the merchant's street-faith lives in the hidden one. The two registers quietly chafe: the clergy find the folk-fervour of smile-claims a touch superstitious, and cannot quite say so aloud, because she does walk.

Her four aspects are Trade, Wealth, Greed, and Generosity. Where each other Lautaran region took a single face of her, Merkavar is her seat and holds all four at once. The daily drama at a stall is the merchant poised between her Greed-face, which says short the stranger, and her Generous-face, which says deal fair to the maybe-goddess, and the market is the proving-ground where the whole of her is weighed in every transaction. Worship keeps no liturgy apart from the deal itself: the open hand is the daily rite, the climb up Helgafjall the rite before a deal of consequence, the market-opening the rite of the season, and the trade is the prayer.

The Four Seasonal Markets

"You are a citizen of Merkavar when you are here, and you govern it when you show up." Merkavar's commercial year runs on four great markets, one per season. Each market is also a seat of the Seasonal Assembly: every holder of a registered trade route, guild certification, or commerce-treaty seat gets a vote on the legislation the clergy have placed before the floor. The character of each market shapes which ancestry-blocs turn out, which legislation passes, and which struggles dominate the public squares.

Spring
Hazi-Azoka
Basque hazi (seed) + azoka (market): "Seed-Market"
Livestock, labour contracts, the year's planting commitments. The smallest of the four; itinerant population is at its lowest, and permanent residents are most over-represented on the assembly floor. The clergy bring controversial certification standards here when they want them passed.
Summer
Lore-Azoka
Basque lore (bloom, flower) + azoka: "Bloom-Market"
Luxury-trade peaks; merchant-houses bring their finest stock to the lake-shore for display and high-value sale. The loudest assembly: floor so crowded with cross-domain visitors that procedural motions fail for want of attention. The clergy bring legislation they want quietly buried here.
Autumn
Uzta-Azoka
Basque uzta (harvest) + azoka: "Harvest-Market"
Bulk grain, iron, and cleared inventory move under the Order of Steam's reduced-fare freight window. Largest halfling turnout of the year; rail crews from three domains are in Merkavar's freight yards anyway. Legislation favouring operators-who-move over artisans-who-make passes reliably in autumn.
Winter
Kontu-Azoka
Basque kontu (account, reckoning) + azoka: "Reckoning-Market"
The smaller permanent population settles accounts, closes long contracts, and trades in high-value goods and information. Kitsune information-brokers do their best winter work here; the year's commercial knowledge consolidates at one table.

Jianna's Clergy · The Standing Administration

Jianna's clergy is Merkavar between the assemblies, which is most of Merkavar. The clergy is cosmopolitan and open to all ancestries: Vishkanya are over-represented (Jianna herself is depicted as a vishkanya, and the precision-instrument and reckoning-machine traditions that supply commercial infrastructure are Vishkanya guild work), but halfling clergy, kitsune clergy, and clergy of every Talanese ancestry serve at every rank, and the church grows out of the whole Commerce population. A halfling rail-conductor who reads the codes well enough to arbitrate a freight-routing dispute can take orders; a kitsune market-reader who has built enough trust to broker an inter-guild settlement can take orders; the gates are commercial competence and the willingness to keep the rites.

The clergy handle commercial law arbitration, road maintenance, weights-and-measures inspection, the agenda for each upcoming assembly, and the institutional continuity that a legislature meeting four times a year cannot provide for itself. They serve Jianna; the well-struck deal, the honest weight, and the fair market are the church's acts of faith. They also do the slower political work of agenda-control, which is the quieter form of governance.

A clergy administrator who wants a new certification standard enacted will draft it, circulate it to partner guilds in the preceding weeks, and bring it to Hazi-Azoka when the itinerant population is smallest. An administrator who wants to block something will time its first reading for Lore-Azoka, when the floor is so loud that procedural motions fail for want of attention. This is not corruption. It is governance by people who are always present, managing a legislature whose members mostly are not, and the line between legitimate institutional knowledge and political manipulation is the subject of the most reliable arguments in Merkavar's public life.

The Halflings and the Trains

The halflings run the trains, and the trains run the assembly. The northern network's reduced-fare freight window, the two-week period each autumn when the Order of Steam drops bulk-cargo rates to clear capacity before the winter schedule, overlaps Uzta-Azoka and draws the largest halfling turnout of any market. Rail crew are in Merkavar's freight yards anyway, between rotations or waiting on return loads; passage discounts for rail workers bring the off-shift ones from three domains over.

The autumn assembly is consequently the largest of the year, reliably dominated by halfling freight operators and rail conductors who vote in compact, well-organised blocs shaped by the Halfling Railworkers' Association. The Association is not a clerical body, not a guild in the Vishkanya sense, and not a Consortium seat-holder. It is a labour-and-civic federation that represents every halfling working a rail job on the northern network's Merkavaran section, and it whips assembly votes with the discipline of a culture that has spent generations coordinating caravan-rotations across continental distances.

The legislation that passes in autumn reflects their priorities: route-maintenance standards, junction-access fees, feeder-license protections, and trade-standard provisions that benefit the operators who move goods more than the artisans who make them. The Order of Steam has set the freight window in the same two-week slot for forty years. Whether this is efficient scheduling or deliberate influence over Merkavar's legislative tone is a question several merchants have asked without finding documentation either way. The clergy have never formally raised it.

The Kitsune and the Information Markets

The kitsune of Merkavar have been here as long as the city has had markets to attend. They are an ancient parallel tradition to the Heartcourt's forested kingdom in the highlands of Emarrea: where Emarrean kitsune built a polycule court and a poetry-and-festival civic life, Merkavar-kitsune built market-stalls and information networks. The two registers diverged so long ago that kitsune from each are sometimes mutually unrecognisable to outsiders, and the diplomatic correspondence between Biozuri and Merkavar is conducted through Talanese rather than through Kotokoe to avoid the constant register-clashes.

Merkavar-kitsune operate in a register that is more commercially pragmatic and less courtly than their Emarrean counterparts would recognise as characteristically kitsune. Their foxfire-lit stalls in the market squares are the ones where you go to find out what you didn't know you needed to know; they are market-readers, information brokers, and the intermediaries who explain to a Vishkanya guild consortium why the halfling bloc voted the way it did, and explain to the halfling bloc what the consortium's counter-offer actually means in commercial-law terms. The clergy hire them as auditors. The Association hires them as outside readers. Visiting merchant-houses hire them as everything.

Their assembly votes are consistent across seasons in ways that suggest coordination, and they resist categorisation in ways that suggest the coordination is genuine and the categorisation would be expensive for whoever attempted it. Their numbers are small relative to the wider Merkavar population; they cannot swing a vote alone, and have never needed to be.

The Deepest Political Question

The relationship between the clergy and the assembly is the deepest political question in Merkavar and one neither institution has formally resolved. The clergy interpret commercial law that the assembly enacts; the line between interpretation and governance is the same line every legal-religious tradition argues about, and Merkavar's version is no easier to settle. An assembly-passed trade standard supersedes prior church arbitration in principle; the clergy's role in shaping what the assembly votes on means the church's interpretations are inscribed in legislation before the legislators ever see it.

Three different arbitration bodies in the last century have produced three different answers to the supremacy question, and none has been accepted as final by both sides. The city runs anyway, because in Merkavar the well-functioning market is the highest civic good, and both clergy and assembly understand that an unresolvable structural argument is a small price for a functioning Market City.

Open in the Chronicle Record

The Market City is older than any chronicle that survives intact, and the chronicle record has long since stopped trying to catalogue it. Several specific questions remain open in canon:

  • City layout and districts. The plaza-pattern is now drawn in outline (travellers' plazas by the freight-yards, local plazas of the permanent quarters, by-trade plazas like the cloth- and grain-plazas), but the named squares, the freight-yard quarter, the temple climb toward Helgafjall, the warehouse rings, the named guildhalls and named clergy-precincts remain canon-pending.
  • Named clergy figures. The senior administrative ranks, the high-priest equivalents who chair the standing committees, the cross-ancestry clergy whose careers exemplify the cosmopolitan ideal: all unnamed.
  • Named Halfling Railworkers' Association leadership. The Association's whip, its chief negotiator, its representative on the assembly floor: characters waiting to be cast.
  • Named kitsune information-brokers. The market-reader whose seasonal stall is the canonical first stop for new merchants, the broker whose contract-arbitration record is the longest unbroken streak on the continent: open.
  • Historical clergy-vs-assembly arbitration cases. The three different supremacy answers of the last century each came from a named arbitration body in a named dispute; the dispute names, the bodies, and the legal reasoning are canon-pending.
  • The four-market traditions. The shape of Merkavar's worship is now drawn (the open hand as the daily rite, the Helgafjall climb before a deal of consequence, the market-opening as the rite of the season, the trade itself as the prayer), but each market's own ritual rhythm remains unwritten: the opening rites, closing rites, the songs sung at dawn on the first day, the customary first toast at the merchant-house dinner.
  • Specific Helgafjall pilgrimage practices. The trails, the way-shrines, the threshold-tokens left at the summit, the prayer-formulae merchant-houses send their apprentices to recite.

Pace these as stories want them. The Market City does not need them all written; it benefits from each being there when called for.

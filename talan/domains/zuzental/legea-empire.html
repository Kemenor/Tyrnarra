The Divine Faith

The Faith venerates a god who is not bound by the Gods' Law. The bound thirteen swore that compact at the close of the Week of Crimson Rain and accepted its constraint as the price of Material-Plane presence; the Faith's god did not. They reside in the third planar layer — Postlife — and operate there, beyond the Ethereal Veil's binding force, with full unconstrained divinity.

What this means in practice: the Faith's god can still affect the Material Plane through chosen mortals, through portfolio-granted clerical power, and through the demigod scion who reigns as Empire. What they cannot do is walk among mortals as the bound thirteen do. The result is a faith whose god is felt everywhere in the Empire — and seen nowhere.

Which specific Postlife plane the god calls home — Elysium (the Good plane), Diyu (the Balance plane), or Abyss (the Evil plane) — is canonically open. Each possibility recasts the Faith in a different doctrinal light, and the Empire's clergy famously decline to confirm which it is when asked. The teaching, they say, is the same in all three.

The Demigod Line

The Empire is ruled by a demigod — half-divine in literal blood, descended from or championed by the Faith's god in a relationship the clergy describe as scion and which neutral scholars describe as uncertain. What is certain: the demigod manifests divine power well beyond mortal scale, lives noticeably longer than a mortal of comparable health, and is recognised by the Faith's god as the throne's rightful occupant.

The line is hereditary. Succession passes from demigod to theocratic prince to next demigod across generations — though scholars outside the Empire are quietly unsure whether the divine inheritance attenuates over time, or whether each heir is renewed through some rite at investiture. The Empire does not publish its theology of succession; pilgrims and ambassadors return with different answers.

What the line does publish, repeatedly, is the doctrine that the demigod's authority is not from the Empire's people but from the god directly. The throne is not a contract with the governed. It is a relay of divine will. This framing matters: it is the doctrinal point on which the Empire most sharply distinguishes itself from Forseti's law, which treats authority as a chain of sworn compacts.

Law Against Law — The Structural Tension

The Empire sits inside Zuzental — Forseti's domain, the domain of law itself. Two divine legal authorities operate inside the same territory: Forseti's law, which is the foundation of Zuzental's continental reputation, and the Faith's law, which the Empire's god claims is older and truer. The boundary between them is a continual political and theological argument that the rest of Talan watches carefully.

The Faith's Position

The bound thirteen swore the Gods' Law as a treaty at the close of the Week of Crimson Rain. A sworn law is a contingent law — agreed to under specific historical pressure, accepted in exchange for a benefit, in principle revocable if the parties chose. Forseti's law is sworn law.

The Faith's god did not swear that law and was not party to that treaty. Their law has always been free — pre-existing, unconditioned, owed to no one. Older. Truer. The Empire's authority follows from that older law.

The Empire teaches this from the pulpit. Forseti's clergy in the Thousand Kingdom find it provocative on theological grounds and unconvincing on legal ones.

Forseti's Position

A law sworn by the parties bound to it is a law given consent. A law imposed from outside — older, freer, owed to no one — is by definition unaccountable. The Faith mistakes antiquity for legitimacy, and the difference matters: the Gods' Law was sworn precisely because the alternative was the petty divine governance of the Gods' Era, which mortals had no say in starting and no power to end.

Forseti's clergy say this less publicly than the Faith says the inverse, but they say it.

That Forseti — the literal god of binding-rules — tolerates the Empire's existence within his own domain is, to outside scholars, the single most interesting fact about the arrangement. Theories range from he is bound by his own law to permit it to he is studying the Empire for reasons of his own to the Empire's god and Forseti have an agreement that neither side has cared to disclose. Forseti has not commented. The Empire reads his silence as deference; the Thousand Kingdom reads it as patience.

An additional open question on the Empire's antiquity: does the Legea Empire predate the Gods' Law, or postdate the Week of Crimson Rain? If it predates, then the Empire's existence inside Zuzental was negotiated into the Gods' Law as a special exception — which would mean Forseti accepted it as a condition of being bound at all. If it postdates, then the Empire grew on Forseti's land afterward, and his tolerance is a choice rather than a treaty obligation. Antiquity TBD

The Missionary Programme

The Empire is not content to be a sovereign theocracy on its own ground. The Faith runs an active missionary programme beyond its borders — sending clergy to other domains, founding small worship-houses in friendly cities, and (most consequentially) penetrating cultures whose existing religious balance is fragile enough to be tipped by a determined outside push.

The most documented case is Fenurra (Ehizahar / Hunt). Faith missionaries have converted a portion of the Fenurran tribes, fracturing the historic tribal balance under which the Speaker's Mantle had passed peaceably for generations. The ruling Draconis tribe — caretakers of the Mantle for many generations — were forced into a precarious position: either fight the conversions outright and risk a Fenurran civil war, or absorb the encroaching ideology into Draconis politics and bend it to their own ends.

They chose absorption. The Draconis arranged the political marriage of their daughter to the Theocratic Prince — the heir to the demigod line. If you cannot fight a religion, marry it. The marriage is now a fact of Talanese diplomacy. The outcome is unsettled. It may unify Fenurra under a new religious overlay. It may shatter the tribal balance entirely. It may drag Fenurra bodily into the law-vs-law theological argument the Empire and Forseti have been conducting at simmering temperature for generations.

Beyond Fenurra, the Empire's missionary footprint is harder to inventory. Quiet worship-houses exist in several Zuzental towns, on the legal pretext that worship under the Faith is permitted so long as it does not contradict Forseti's law (which the Empire argues is the trivially-true case). Whether the missionary work reaches further — to Ezkudon, to Lautara, to the Free City — is rumour, not record.

⌬   Open in Canon

The Empire is a canon sub-region with deliberately unfinished identity. Future canon work will close these in turn; until then, the TBD state is the world's state, not an oversight in the writing.
  1. Which Postlife plane houses the Faith's god — Elysium, Diyu, or Abyss. Each recasts the Empire's moral colour.
  2. The god's name and identity — title-only at present ("the god of the Faith").
  3. The reigning demigod's name and nature — half-divine scion, but the specifics of the divine inheritance and personal identity are open.
  4. The Theocratic Prince's personal name — the heir who now carries the Draconis marriage and the future of the line.
  5. Antiquity — whether the Empire predates the Gods' Law (negotiated into it as a special exception) or postdates the Week of Crimson Rain (grew on Forseti's land afterward). Reshapes the law-vs-law tension significantly.
  6. Doctrinal contrast in practice — exactly how the Faith's legal doctrine differs from Forseti's at the level of daily kingdom law (criminal, contract, succession, oath). The teachings collide in principle; the practical case-law collisions are not yet written.

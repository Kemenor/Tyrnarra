Read Aloud to Players
Read Aloud

You smell Millhaven before you see it. Salt and woodsmoke from the west, bread and horses from the east, and underneath both — river water, cold and constant, carrying everything the town touches down to the sea.

The town sits on both banks of the Millwater River, where it narrows before its final run to Midarra. The western bank is the Cape side — older, saltier, streets worn smooth by generations of fisherfolk and sailors who never quite made it back to wherever they came from. The buildings lean slightly toward the water, as if thinking about it. On the eastern bank is the Realm side, wider-streeted and better-kept, smelling of the grain warehouses that line the market square and the shrine to Sarrum that has stood there since before anyone's grandfather was born.

Between them, straddling the river on three bridges that were never meant to hold this many buildings, is everything else. Shops and stalls and offices and taverns, all stacked and cantilevered over the water, connected by staircases that don't quite meet and walkways that technically belong to no one. The Millwater runs dark beneath it all, unbothered.

A word on the name. The river is called the Millwater because Millhaven is built on it. Millhaven is called Millhaven because it was built at the Millwater. Historians have argued about which came first — the mill, the water, or the haven — for approximately as long as the town has existed, and show no signs of stopping. Locals find this very funny. Visitors find it less funny the third time someone brings it up.

On the widest of the three bridges, above a sign that swings in the river wind, is the office you're looking for. A compass rose, painted gold, slightly faded. The Wayward Compass — Adventurers Guild, Branch 1,847. The door is open. It usually is.

Millhaven is a town that has survived everything it's been asked to survive and profited from most of it. It is warm, and loud, and has opinions about everything. It will probably have opinions about you, before long. Welcome.

GM Tone Notes

Millhaven is cheerful about its own chaos. The three-faction council, the pirate ships at the Cape docks, the temporal weirdness in the Drift Quarter — locals treat all of it as normal. They've built a working town out of contradictions and they're quietly proud of it. The party should feel like they've landed somewhere alive, not somewhere waiting to be rescued.

The river is the spine. Every district has a relationship with the Millwater — the Cape works it, the Realm sells across it, the bridges live on it. When in doubt, put a scene near the water. It gives you ambient noise, movement, and the ever-present reminder that Midarra is right there at the end of it.

The council conflict is background radiation, not a main plot. It should be felt — in overheard arguments, in which merchants are friendly and which aren't, in the Watch's visible frustration — but the party shouldn't be asked to solve it in session one. Let it simmer. Let them notice it themselves.

The Tani drift is subtle. Don't lead with it. Let a player notice that a fisherman seems confused about what day it is, or that the clocks on the Cape side don't quite match the ones on the Realm side. The Drift Quarter is there when they go looking. They don't need to go looking yet.

The Town

Millhaven grew the way most good things do — by accident. A mill was built where the Millwater River narrowed before its run to Midarra. A waystation followed. Then a market. Then, when sailors discovered the river mouth gave them shelter from the central sea's chop, a dock. Then another dock. Then a second market to serve the first dock's sailors, and then someone built a tavern on the bridge between the two sides and realized they were printing coin.

The town sits precisely on the border between the Dreaming Cape and the Earth Realm — and precisely on neither. The Cape side smells of salt, woodsmoke, and something faintly strange that locals call "the Tani drift," a mild temporal blurring that manifests mostly as déjà vu, the occasional misremembered morning, and fishermen who swear they've pulled the same net twice. The Realm side smells of grain, horses, and fresh bread. The bridge houses smell of opportunity.

Millhaven is warm, hardworking, and deeply resistant to being told what to do by anyone not from here — which includes the three factions on its own council. It has survived storms, a pirate blockade, two merchant wars, and one very confused temporal echo that lasted three days and is never discussed. It will survive whatever the party brings to it, and probably profit from it.

Strategic Position
Why Millhaven Matters
The Millwater River gives direct access to Midarra and the central sea lanes. Rika Tikur, the independent city, sits a short coastal sail to the west — close enough that its ships are a common sight at the Cape docks. The Order of Steam's volcanic southern territory is reachable by sea as well, hugging the coast southwest. The Earth Realm to the east feeds the continent's breadbasket trade routes. Millhaven sits at the intersection of all of it — which is exactly why the Adventurers Guild put a branch here.
Magic & Services

As a settlement of its size, Millhaven supports two dedicated magic practitioners: Oswin Thetch, a wizard who runs a shop called The Empirical Eye on the Cape side, dealing in scrolls, components, and minor enchantments; and Sister Margot of Shuun, a water cleric whose healing house doubles as the Cape's de facto temple. The Realm side has a shrine to Sarrum tended by a lay priest and a travelling cleric of Jianna who comes through on market days.

Uncommon items can be sourced with a few days' notice. Rare items require sending to Rika Tikur or waiting for the right ship. Nothing legendary is available locally — but locals know someone who knows someone.

The Three Faces of Millhaven

Ask someone where they're from in Millhaven and they'll say "the Cape," "the Realm," or "the bridges" — never just "Millhaven." The three districts are distinct enough that they feel like separate towns that agreed, grudgingly, to share a name.

The Cape Side
Dreaming Cape
The older half of Millhaven, built on ground that technically belongs to Tani's domain. The architecture is weathered wood and river stone, the streets narrow and salt-crusted. A faint Tani drift runs through it — nothing dramatic, but locals set their clocks twice a day and don't make firm plans past noon. The fishing fleet docks here. So do the ships that don't advertise their cargo.
The Sodden MastRoughest tavern in town. Best fish stew. Pirates welcome if they pay.
The Empirical EyeOswin Thetch's magic shop. Cluttered, reliable, oddly competitive on price.
Sister Margot's HouseHealing house & temple of Shuun. Also the Cape's unofficial dispute resolution.

The Bridge Houses
Neither · Both
Three bridges span the Millwater, and over two centuries the buildings built onto them have merged, stacked, and sprawled until they're effectively a floating district. No district tax applies here — the Cape claims the eastern supports, the Realm claims the western, and the bridges themselves are famously unresolved. The Wayward Compass guild office sits on the widest central bridge. Coincidence, they say.

More recently, the sprawl has stopped pretending to need the bridges at all. Pilings have been driven into the riverbed between the spans, and on those pilings sit sheds, then storerooms, then someone's home, then a surprisingly respectable tea house. Two private docks hang off the central structure — not quite Cape, not quite Realm, not quite bridge — accessible only by river skiff or by climbing down from the Low Span. The Watch has no jurisdiction here. The Cape dockmaster claims she does. The people who use those docks disagree with both of them, and the river doesn't have an opinion.

The Realm Side
Earth Realm
Wider streets, taller buildings, a proper market square. The Realm side is where the money is counted and the grain is stored. It feels more prosperous and more boring than the Cape — which is exactly how its residents like it. The Merchant Council's formal meeting house is here. So is the Millhaven Watch, who have jurisdiction over exactly this side and are quietly furious about the bridges.
Sarrum's StoneShrine and lay-temple to Sarrum. Ancient, well-maintained, genuinely beloved.

The Grain ExchangeWhere the farming wealth of the Earth Realm comes to be weighed and priced.

The Merchant Council of Millhaven

Nineteen seats. Three factions. No permanent majority. The Council governs Millhaven through a combination of consensus, economic leverage, and occasional spectacular shouting matches in the Council Hall. Decisions require ten votes. Earth holds eight, Cape holds eight — one short each, always. The Bridge Merchants hold three, and every single vote they cast is a political event. To pass anything, a major faction needs at least two of the three bridge seats — which means the bridge bloc negotiates internally before it negotiates externally, and nothing is ever simple.

The Current Crisis
A ship carrying a combined grain-and-cargo shipment — backed by both Earth and Cape council money — went missing three weeks ago on the Midarra run. The Earth faction blames Cape negligence. The Cape faction blames a rival out of Rika Tikur. The bridge merchants have called for an independent investigation and everyone suspects they already know what happened.

The Veldtmark Invitation
Someone stopped answering. Someone always stops answering.
Party Level 5 3–4 Sessions Cape Side — Tani Drift Zone Investigation + Combat Multiple Endings
The Contract

The contract on the board is unremarkable at first glance — a welfare check on a local wealthy resident who has failed to attend several scheduled appointments. It was posted by his business partner, who is owed a significant sum and is beginning to worry. The pay is modest. The expected difficulty is listed by Kael as "social — low risk."

Kael rated it before he thought about it too hard. He hasn't changed the rating. He has, however, mentioned to Mira Seld that something about the posting feels off to him. She agreed. Neither of them has said this to the party.

Read Aloud — Quest Board
"Lord Caevan Veldtmark, merchant and landowner of the Cape district, has failed to respond to correspondence or attend any scheduled appointments for the past three weeks. His business partner, one Harlen Doss, requests a welfare check and, if the lord is well, collection of a debt of record. Inquire at the Wayward Compass for details. Discretion expected."
GM Note — What Harlen Doss Actually Knows
Harlen Doss is genuinely worried about his money but also genuinely worried about Veldtmark. The last time they spoke, three weeks ago, Veldtmark was distracted and strange — kept referring to "the great work" and "the invitation being answered." Harlen assumed he was drunk. He's been assuming that ever since because the alternative is worse. If the party presses him, he'll admit this. He won't volunteer it.
Background — Lord Caevan Veldtmark

Caevan Veldtmark is a merchant lord of middling wealth and significant social ambition. He inherited the Cape estate from his father, expanded it with trade profits, and built the small church on the grounds approximately eight months ago — ostensibly as a private chapel dedicated to Tani, god of Time, which fits the Cape district's temporal character and raised no suspicion.

The truth is that Veldtmark has been a member of a secretive philosophical society for years — one obsessed with what lies beneath the Tani drift, what the temporal disturbance on the Cape actually touches, and whether something on the other side of it can be contacted. Six months ago, they made contact. Four months ago, something answered. Three months ago, Veldtmark stopped attending social functions. The church is not dedicated to Tani.

The Thing That Answered
It has no name that mortals have recorded. It exists in the space that the Tani drift brushes against — not the past or the future, but the space between moments, the gap where time doesn't flow. It is not a demon. It is not a vampire. It is something that was old before either category existed. It experiences hunger the way time experiences forward motion: constantly, inevitably, without cruelty but also without mercy. Veldtmark offered it worship, feeding, and a vessel. It accepted. It is currently contained in the basement because containment was part of the original bargain — until it is strong enough not to need the bargain anymore.
Getting There
Read Aloud — The Road to Veldtmark Estate
The estate sits on the Cape side's northern edge, where the salt-crusted streets thin out and the Tani drift sits heavier in the air. You've walked this road before. You know you haven't — this is your first time here — but the certainty keeps surfacing, quiet and wrong, like a word you can't stop almost remembering. The iron gate is closed but not locked. The grounds beyond it are well-kept. Too well-kept, perhaps, for a man who hasn't been seen in three weeks. The gravel path to the front door is raked perfectly smooth. There are no footprints in it. Not even the gardener's.
GM Note — The Drift
From the moment the party enters the gate, light Tani drift effects apply: persistent déjà vu, a sense that they have done each action seconds before doing it, and occasionally a fractional delay — a sound that arrives slightly before or after its source. These are disorienting but not mechanically harmful. Increase the intensity as they approach the basement. By the time they reach the ritual chamber, the drift is strong enough that they may see brief ghost-images of past events in the estate — fleeting, silent, gone before they can examine them. Don't explain this. Let them ask questions.
The Butler
Read Aloud — The Front Door
The door opens before you knock. The man standing in it is tall, precisely dressed, and the color of old bone. Not pale — bone. His eyes are the grey of something that has stopped having preferences. He inclines his head at exactly the angle required by courtesy and no further. "The lord is not receiving visitors," he says. His voice is correct in every technical respect. "If you have business with the estate, I am empowered to take messages." He has not blinked since the door opened.
GM Note — Aldous, The Butler
Aldous is not dead. He is not undead. He has simply been in proximity to the Thing long enough that something has been... smoothed away. He was a perfectly normal man eight months ago. He will answer questions politely and deflect them precisely. He will not lie — he will use technically true statements to mislead. He knows the party is here because of the debt. He will offer to write a bank draft. If the party insists on seeing Veldtmark personally, he will "consult the lord's schedule" and return to say the lord can see them tomorrow. He is trying to make them leave. He is not threatening. He is deeply, quietly wrong, and players who succeed on Perception or Insight checks will feel it even if they can't name it.
GM Note — Getting Inside
Social entry works if the party is persistent or clever — Aldous will eventually allow them to "wait in the sitting room" while he checks on the lord, which gives them access to the ground floor. They can also find their way in through the church on the grounds (unlocked, "open to worshippers"), through a window, or by following a staff member who is running an errand outside. The estate does not want to seem like it's hiding anything. That's more important to Aldous than keeping them out.

The Basement — Entry Points
Four Hidden Entrances
The basement is accessible through four concealed entry points on the ground floor. Each requires discovery — none are obvious. The party is unlikely to find all four; they only need one. Each entry delivers them to a different part of the basement and colors their initial experience of it differently.
Entry 1 — The Church (Right Side of Grounds)
Behind the altar, a stone prayer rail slides aside when pressed in the correct sequence — the sequence is written in the church's hymnal in a cipher using Tani's holy symbols, which a Religion check will crack. This entry deposits the party into the sarcophagus corridor. They'll hear the ritual chamber before they see it.
Entry 2 — The Organ Room (Top Left)
Playing a specific chord sequence on the organ — the last piece of sheet music on the stand, which is not a real composition — opens a passage behind the instrument. A Performance or Investigation check identifies the sequence. This entry leads directly to the pipe corridor above the barred cell. They'll hear something in the cell before they're ready for it.
Entry 3 — The Well (Between Church and Mansion)
The well is dry. It has always been dry — it was never a real well. Iron rungs lead down to a passage that connects to the winding path between the ritual chamber and the sarcophagus corridor. This is the most direct route and the most exposed — no cover once they're in the main passage.
Entry 4 — Locked Storage, Kitchen (Top Center)
A locked door in the kitchen storage area — Aldous has the key, or a Thievery check opens it — leads to a narrow servants' stair down to the pipe corridor near the ritual chamber's north wall. The party can hear the ritual chanting clearly from this entry. If they haven't encountered cultists yet, three of them are in the corridor below, preparing an offering.

Cast of the Estate
What Is Actually Happening

Eight months ago, Lord Veldtmark made contact — through rituals his philosophical society had been refining for years — with something that exists in the space the Tani drift brushes against. Not a spirit. Not a god. Something that experiences time differently than anything else alive, because it exists between moments rather than within them.

The contact was intoxicating. The thing communicated through sensation rather than words — a feeling of total significance, of being seen across all of time simultaneously. Veldtmark interpreted this as communion. It was closer to the attention of something very large noticing something very small.

The bargain was Veldtmark's idea. He offered the Thing a vessel, worship, and regular feeding — human vitality, drawn through the cult rituals — in exchange for what he called "the gift of true sight." He wanted to see across time the way it does. Instead, he got something closer to standing too close to a furnace: he can sense temporal echoes now, see ghost-images of past moments, feel the future as a pressure behind his eyes. It is destroying him slowly. He believes it is transcendence. He is incorrect.

The Thing in the coffin is not imprisoned. The chains are ceremonial. It remains in the basement because it is not yet strong enough to move freely in mortal time — the feeding has been irregular, the cult is small, and the bargain includes a clause Veldtmark doesn't fully understand: it cannot act until invited above the threshold by the person who made the original pact. Veldtmark has not yet done this. He is saving it for what he calls "the completion."

What "The Completion" Means
Veldtmark believes that when the Thing is strong enough, he will formally invite it above the threshold, it will grant him true temporal sight, and they will exist in a partnership of mutual elevation. What will actually happen: the Thing will step above the threshold, the bargain will be technically fulfilled, and it will no longer be bound by any of its terms. It will feed freely, move freely, and Veldtmark will be its first unstructured meal. It has no malice toward him. It simply won't need the arrangement anymore.
How Strong Is It Currently?
Approximately 60–70% of what it needs to operate freely above the threshold. At current feeding rates it would reach completion in six to eight weeks. The party has arrived at a point where the situation is still containable — barely. If they do nothing and leave, they will be back within two months for something considerably worse.
How This Can End

There is no single correct resolution. The party's choices determine the outcome. Each ending has consequences that can ripple into future sessions.

The Letter — Found on Harlen Doss
GM Note
This letter was sent by Veldtmark to Harlen six weeks ago — before the silence, while he still had enough of himself left to write coherently. Harlen has not opened it. If the party convinces him to hand it over, or find it on his person, this is what they read. It is the clearest single document in the adventure. It is also heartbreaking if the party has already met Veldtmark, because they will recognize the man who wrote it is still in there somewhere, buried under six more weeks of the Thing's attention. This letter is the key to the "Save Veldtmark" ending — showing it to him, particularly the final paragraph, is the crack in his framework that everything else can follow through.
Read Aloud — The Letter
Veldtmark Estate · Cape District · 6 weeks prior

Harlen,

I am writing this in the morning, which is when I am most myself. I have noticed that I am less myself as the day progresses. I thought you should know that I have noticed this, because it means part of me is still paying attention, and I find that reassuring even if I suspect I should not.

I want to tell you that the work is going well, because it is, by every measure I have established for it. The contact is stronger. The communion deepens every week. What I have described to you as the great work is proceeding exactly as I hoped when I began it. I am not writing to tell you it has gone wrong.

I am writing because I had a dream last night in which I was myself at twenty-three, sitting in the study with my father, and my father asked me what I wanted from my life, and I said I wanted to understand things. He said that was a fine ambition. In the dream I knew he was going to die in three years and I didn't tell him. I woke up and I couldn't remember the last time I thought about my father.

I couldn't remember the last time I thought about anything that wasn't the work.

The debt I owe you — the shipping agreement from last autumn — I am enclosing a draft for the full amount. You should have it regardless of how things proceed. You have been a good partner and a good friend, and I am aware that I have not been available to you in recent months, and I am sorry for that in a way I am finding it difficult to fully articulate.

I believe what I am doing is important. I believe it is the most important thing anyone has done in this city in a very long time, possibly ever. I believe I am on the threshold of something that will change what it means to understand time and existence and the space between moments. I believe all of this completely.

I am also, Harlen — and I am asking you to keep this between us, as I have asked nothing else of you — I am also afraid. I don't know what I'm afraid of exactly. It isn't failure. I think it might be success. I think it might be that I will get exactly what I asked for and discover that what I asked for and what I wanted are not the same thing, and that by the time I know the difference it will be too late to say so.

I am most myself in the mornings. I am writing this down so I don't forget that I knew it.

Give my regards to your wife. Come to dinner when this is over — I'll have Aldous prepare something good. I owe you a proper meal and a proper conversation, the kind we used to have before I became so thoroughly occupied with becoming something other than what I am.

Your friend,
Caevan

P.S. — The draft is enclosed. If you don't hear from me for more than a month, please don't come yourself. Send someone. Send several someones. I am telling you this as a precaution and I am nearly certain it is unnecessary.

GM Notes — Using This Letter

Three moments in this letter do the most work at the table. The first: "I am most myself in the mornings." If the party reads this aloud to Veldtmark, watch his face. He wrote it. He remembers writing it. He has been telling himself for six weeks that he was being dramatic.

The second: the postscript. He knew. Six weeks ago he knew well enough to write a warning into a letter he told himself was a precaution. The party can point out that the precaution was correct and that he left it with the one person he told not to come himself.

The third — and this one is for the party, not Veldtmark: "I think it might be that I will get exactly what I asked for and discover that what I asked for and what I wanted are not the same thing." He diagnosed himself perfectly and proceeded anyway. That's the tragedy. That's also what makes the "Save Veldtmark" ending feel earned rather than easy — he is not a fool who didn't see it coming. He is a man who saw it coming and walked toward it, and the question the party has to answer is whether that makes him more or less worth saving.

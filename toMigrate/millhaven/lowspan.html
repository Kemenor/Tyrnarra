The Establishment

The Low Span started as a fighting ring in the southernmost bridge's undercroft about forty years ago — two dockworkers settling a disagreement in front of an audience that was supposed to be three people and became thirty. Someone started taking bets. Someone else started charging admission. By the time anyone thought to ask who owned it, ownership had become a complicated question answered by three different names on three different documents, none of which are Sable Rei.

What it is now: three levels of descending legitimacy beneath the bridge's public face. The ring on the ground floor is completely above board — agreed rules, consenting fighters, betting conducted openly and taxed, theoretically, by the bridge district's self-appointed revenue collector. Below that, through a trapdoor behind the bar, a second floor where the rules are fewer and the stakes are higher. Below that, a third level that isn't on any document and where the word "below" starts to feel more like a moral description than a physical one.

Sable Rei doesn't own it. Sable Rei is simply always there when something important happens, which is a different kind of ownership entirely.

Read Aloud — Arriving
First Visit
You smell it before you find it — sawdust and river damp and the particular kind of noise that only happens when a crowd is watching something they've put money on. The entrance is a door set into the bridge's south face, unmarked except for a small brass compass rose scratched into the wood at eye level. Inside, the ceiling is the underside of the bridge itself — raw timber and iron bolts — and the floor drops away in tiers toward a sand circle thirty feet across, lit by oil lamps on chains that sway with the river movement. The lamps flicker in sequence. Nobody has explained why.
The Crowd
The crowd is three deep at the rail on the best tiers. Cape dock workers, bridge merchants, two people who are clearly neither and trying to look like both. A halfling woman is taking bets from a leather satchel, moving through the crowd with the efficiency of someone who has memorized everyone's face and their credit. Nobody looks at you when you walk in. In a place like this, not being looked at is a form of welcome.
GM Note — The Compass Rose
The compass rose scratched into the door is not the Low Span's mark — it's the Wayward Compass guild symbol, very small, done with a knife rather than a tool. It was put there by Aldric Fenn approximately fifteen years ago. He has never mentioned this to anyone. Sable Rei noticed it within a week and has never mentioned it either. It means: the guild knows this place exists, has always known, and has chosen not to ask questions. This is a mutual arrangement. It has served everyone adequately.
Three Floors Down

Each level is accessible from the one above — the transitions are not secret, exactly, but they are not advertised. First-timers stay on the ground floor. Return visitors find the trapdoor. What's below that finds you, when it wants to.

Tonight's Card

The ring runs three to five bouts a night, dusk to midnight, with a late card on Fridays that goes until the lamps run low. The fighters are independent — they contract per bout, take a cut of the betting pool, and are responsible for their own medical arrangements. The Low Span provides the sand, the lamps, and the crowd. It does not provide opinions on outcomes.

Sable Rei always bets on whoever is more interesting, regardless of odds. This is known, frequently imitated, and almost as frequently regretted by imitators who lack Sable Rei's specific judgment about what interesting means in a fight.

GM Note — The Fights as Scene Texture
The ring is not a combat encounter — it's atmosphere and timing. Use the fight in the background of the Sable Rei conversation to control pacing: a good hit from the crowd's reaction gives Sable a moment to think before answering a hard question; the end of a bout is a natural scene break. If the party wants to bet, let them — the odds above are the current card, outcomes are GM's discretion. Sable always wins their bet. This is not luck.
Regulars & Staff
What the Low Span Actually Is

The fighting ring is real. The gambling is real. Everything below the second floor is also real, and considerably more interesting, and the party will only access it if they are invited or very clever or both.

🔒 The Second Floor — The Real Betting Room
Through the trapdoor behind the bar, down a ladder that was not built for people in a hurry, a second room runs the length of the bridge underbelly. Card tables, a second ring with no rules written anywhere, and a bar that stocks things the upstairs bar doesn't acknowledge carrying. The second floor is where bridge district business actually happens — contracts discussed at card tables, information exchanged in the language of raised bets and folded hands. Sable Rei has a standing table here that is never officially reserved and always available.
🔒 The Third Level — The River Room
The third level is not a room. It is a platform over the Millwater itself, accessible only through a door in the second floor's south wall that requires a key nobody admits to having. The platform has a mooring point, a waterproof document locker built into the piling, and enough space for six people to have a conversation that the river current ensures no one above can hear. This is where Sable Rei conducts business that cannot be discussed anywhere else. The party will not access this level in a first visit. They will, eventually, be invited here — and the invitation will mean something significant has changed in how Sable Rei regards them.
🔒 The Deed Question
The Low Span's deed is held in three names: a retired Cape dockmaster named Holt who died eight years ago and whose name was never removed, a woman named Cress who operates a candle shop on the Realm side and has never visited the Low Span in her life and receives a small quarterly payment she has never questioned, and a guild holding entity registered under Wayward Compass Branch #1,847 that Aldric Fenn signed into existence fifteen years ago and has not mentioned to anyone including his own staff. The guild technically co-owns the Low Span. This has never come up. It will, eventually.
🔒 What Flows Through Here
Information, primarily. The Low Span is the best place in Millhaven to learn something — not because anyone tells you things, but because everyone in the bridge district passes through eventually and people talk when they're watching a fight. Vara Mink sends someone on Tuesdays to sit at the second-floor bar and listen. Dova Crestmark has never visited but receives a written summary every two weeks from a source she has never named to anyone. Kael Orvaine comes once a month to watch the fights and does not speak to anyone and leaves before the headliner. Nobody has asked him about this.
